Cypripedium calceolus is a perennial plant with a creeping rhizome. It has 3-4 broad, pleated leaves and produces 1-2 large flowers with a distinctive slipper-shaped pouch. The flowers are typically yellow to maroon in color with twisted petals.

Distinguishing Features

  • Distinctive slipper-shaped labellum (pouch)
  • Twisted, ribbon-like petals
  • Pleated, ovate-lanceolate leaves
  • Flowers range from 5-8 cm in size

Conservation Status

Listed as Endangered on the IUCN Red List due to habitat loss and over-collection. Protected under CITES Appendix II.

Threats

  • Habitat destruction from urbanization
  • Illegal collection for horticultural trade
  • Herbivory by increasing deer populations
  • Climate change affecting alpine habitats

Conservation Efforts

Protected in most European countries. Several ex-situ conservation programs exist, including seed banking and propagation initiatives.

Natural Habitat

Found in open woodlands, scrub, and limestone pavements. Prefers calcareous soils in partial shade at elevations between 500-2000 meters.

Distribution Map

European and Northern Asian distribution

Associated Species

  • Fagus sylvatica (European Beech)
  • Pinus sylvestris (Scots Pine)
  • Vaccinium myrtillus (Bilberry)
  • Dryopteris filix-mas (Male Fern)

Traditional Uses

Historically used in folk medicine as a sedative and nerve tonic. The root was prepared as a tea to treat anxiety, insomnia, and menstrual pain. However, modern use is discouraged due to conservation concerns.

Note: Collection from the wild is illegal in most countries. This species is difficult to cultivate and should not be harvested.

Chemical Constituents

Contains cypripedin (a phenanthrenequinone) and various terpenoids. May cause dermatitis in sensitive individuals.
